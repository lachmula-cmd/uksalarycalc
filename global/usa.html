How US Federal Income Tax Works

The United States uses a progressive federal income tax system with seven tax brackets. Your employer withholds federal income tax from each paycheck based on your W-4 form. The final amount is reconciled when you file your annual federal tax return (Form 1040) by April 15.

Before applying tax brackets, most taxpayers deduct the standard deduction ($14,600 for single filers in 2024) from their gross income. This reduces the amount of income subject to federal tax.

In addition to federal income tax, employees pay FICA (Federal Insurance Contributions Act) taxes: Social Security (6.2%) and Medicare (1.45%). These fund retirement and healthcare benefits.

State taxes not included: Most US states also levy income tax, ranging from 0% (Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, Wyoming) to 13.3% (California). Your actual take-home pay will be lower in most states.

FICA Contributions (Social Security & Medicare)

FICA taxes are separate from federal income tax and are withheld from every paycheck:

Social Security (OASDI)
6.2%
Funds retirement, disability, and survivor benefits. Capped at the Social Security wage base — $168,600 for 2024. Earnings above this are not subject to Social Security tax.
Medicare (Hospital Insurance)
1.45%
Funds Medicare hospital insurance. No wage base cap — applies to all earnings. An additional 0.9% applies to earnings above $200,000 (single filer).

Total employee FICA rate: 7.65% (up to the Social Security wage base). Employers match this amount — they also pay 7.65% on your behalf.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the standard deduction for 2024?

The standard deduction for 2024 is $14,600 for single filers, $29,200 for married filing jointly, and $21,900 for head of household. It reduces your taxable income before applying federal tax brackets.

What is FICA?

FICA (Federal Insurance Contributions Act) taxes fund Social Security and Medicare. Employees pay 6.2% for Social Security (capped at $168,600 in 2024) and 1.45% for Medicare (no cap). Employers match these contributions.

Why is my marginal rate different from my effective rate?

Your marginal rate is the rate on your highest dollar of income. Your effective rate is the average rate across all your income. Because the US uses progressive brackets, only the income above each threshold is taxed at the higher rate — lower income is still taxed at lower rates.

Do I need to file a federal tax return?

Most US residents with income above the standard deduction are required to file a federal tax return (Form 1040) by April 15 each year. Your employer withholds estimated tax throughout the year, and the return reconciles the actual amount owed.
