UAE Salary Guide 2024 — No Personal Income Tax

The United Arab Emirates has no personal income tax for employees. For most expat workers, your gross salary is your take-home pay. This guide explains what deductions may apply, how the UAE tax system works, and what to consider when comparing UAE salaries to other countries.

UAE Tax System — What You Need to Know

The United Arab Emirates is one of the few countries in the world with no personal income tax on employment income. This applies to both UAE nationals and expatriate workers. The UAE government funds public services primarily through oil revenues, VAT, and corporate taxes rather than personal income tax.

0% Personal Income Tax
5% VAT Rate
9% Corporate Tax (2023+)
5% GPSSA (Nationals only)

What taxes exist in the UAE?

  • VAT (Value Added Tax): Introduced in 2018 at 5%. Applies to most goods and services. This affects your cost of living but is not deducted from your salary.
  • Corporate Tax: A 9% corporate tax was introduced in June 2023 for businesses with profits above AED 375,000. This does not affect employee salaries.
  • Excise Tax: Applied to tobacco (100%), energy drinks (100%), and carbonated drinks (50%). Not a salary deduction.
  • No capital gains tax, no inheritance tax, no wealth tax.

GPSSA — Pension for UAE Nationals

UAE nationals working in the private sector are enrolled in the General Pension and Social Security Authority (GPSSA) scheme. This is the only mandatory salary deduction for UAE citizens in private sector employment.

GPSSA — Employee Contribution (UAE Nationals)
Mandatory pension contribution for UAE nationals in private sector employment. Funds the state pension scheme. Expatriate employees are not enrolled and pay nothing.
5%
GPSSA — Employer Contribution (UAE Nationals)
Employer pays an additional 12.5% for UAE national employees. This is not deducted from the employee's salary.
12.5%
Expatriates: Expat workers (the vast majority of the UAE workforce) are not enrolled in GPSSA and have no mandatory social security deductions. Some employers offer voluntary end-of-service gratuity schemes or private pension plans, but these are not mandatory deductions from salary.

End-of-Service Gratuity (EOSB)

While not a salary deduction, UAE labour law requires employers to pay an end-of-service gratuity (EOSB) to employees who complete at least one year of service. This is a significant benefit unique to the UAE and GCC region.

Years of Service Gratuity Entitlement Notes
1–5 years 21 days' basic salary per year Based on last drawn basic salary
5+ years 30 days' basic salary per year For years beyond 5
Less than 1 year Not entitled Minimum 1 year required
Important: Gratuity is calculated on basic salary only — not total package (which may include housing, transport, and other allowances). The total gratuity is capped at 2 years' total salary. Gratuity is paid on leaving the company, not monthly.

UAE Salary Structure — Understanding Your Package

UAE employment packages are often structured differently from European salaries. Understanding the components is important for comparing offers and calculating your true take-home pay.

Basic Salary
The core component of your salary. Used to calculate gratuity, overtime, and GPSSA contributions. Typically 40–60% of total package. Lower basic salary = lower gratuity entitlement.
Housing Allowance
A common component of UAE packages, often 20–30% of total salary. Some employers provide accommodation directly instead. Not subject to any tax.
Transport Allowance
Typically AED 1,000–3,000/month. Covers commuting costs. Some employers provide a company car instead.
Medical Insurance
Mandatory for employers to provide in Dubai and Abu Dhabi. Usually employer-paid and not deducted from salary. Quality varies significantly by employer.
Compare total packages carefully: A UAE salary of AED 200,000 with housing included is very different from AED 200,000 without housing. Dubai rents can be AED 60,000–150,000+ per year for a decent apartment. Always clarify what is included in your package.

Comparing UAE Salaries to UK, EU, and Global Salaries

The UAE's zero income tax makes direct salary comparisons with other countries complex. A UK salary of £80,000 might yield £55,000 net after tax and National Insurance. The same purchasing power in the UAE would require a much lower gross salary — but cost of living, especially housing, must be factored in.

Key Facts About Working in the UAE

  • The UAE dirham (AED) is pegged to the US dollar at a fixed rate of 3.6725 AED/USD, providing currency stability.
  • The UAE has no personal income tax, capital gains tax, inheritance tax, or wealth tax.
  • VAT at 5% applies to most goods and services since January 2018.
  • Corporate tax at 9% applies to business profits above AED 375,000 (from June 2023).
  • Expatriates make up approximately 88–90% of the UAE workforce.
  • The UAE has free zones (DIFC, ADGM, JAFZA, etc.) with their own regulatory frameworks and sometimes different employment rules.
  • There is no minimum wage for most private sector workers (though some sectors have guidelines).
  • The UAE introduced a new labour law in 2022 (Federal Decree-Law No. 33 of 2021) with significant changes to employment contracts and worker protections.
  • Employees in the UAE are not entitled to unemployment benefits — there is no state unemployment insurance for expats.

Frequently Asked Questions — UAE Tax and Salary

Does the UAE have income tax?

No. The UAE has no personal income tax on employment income. This applies to both UAE nationals and expatriate workers. Your gross salary is effectively your net take-home pay (subject to any voluntary deductions or GPSSA for UAE nationals). This has been the case since the UAE's founding and there are no current plans to introduce personal income tax.

Do I need to pay tax in my home country on UAE income?

This depends on your home country's tax rules and your residency status. UK citizens who are non-UK tax residents generally do not pay UK tax on UAE earnings. However, if you remain UK tax resident, you may owe UK tax on worldwide income. US citizens are taxed on worldwide income regardless of where they live. Always consult a tax adviser in your home country before relocating.

What is the end-of-service gratuity?

UAE labour law requires employers to pay a gratuity (lump sum) when an employee leaves after completing at least one year of service. The amount is 21 days' basic salary per year for the first 5 years, and 30 days' basic salary per year thereafter. It is paid on resignation, redundancy, or end of contract — not monthly. It is calculated on basic salary only, not total package.

Is the UAE expensive to live in?

Dubai and Abu Dhabi are among the more expensive cities in the world, particularly for housing, international schooling, and healthcare. However, everyday costs like food, transport, and utilities can be reasonable. The zero income tax significantly boosts purchasing power compared to high-tax countries, but housing costs can absorb much of that advantage — especially for families requiring international schools.

How does UAE take-home pay compare to the UK or Germany?

Because there is no income tax, UAE gross salary equals net salary for expats. A UAE salary of AED 200,000 (≈ £43,000) is fully take-home. In the UK, you would need a gross salary of approximately £62,000–65,000 to achieve the same net pay. However, UK employees benefit from NHS healthcare, state pension, and other social protections not available in the UAE. See our guide on why net pay differs by country.

Will the UAE ever introduce income tax?

There are no current plans to introduce personal income tax in the UAE. The government has stated that personal income tax is not being considered. The introduction of corporate tax in 2023 and VAT in 2018 shows the UAE is diversifying revenue sources, but personal income tax remains off the table as of 2024.
