How Dutch Income Tax Works

The Netherlands uses a box system for income tax (inkomstenbelasting). Employment income falls under Box 1 (work and home). A key feature of the Dutch system is that social contributions are included within the Box 1 tax rates — unlike most other countries where they are calculated separately.

Box 1 has two bands: 36.97% up to €75,518, and 49.5% above that. These rates already include contributions to the state pension (AOW), survivor's benefit (ANW), and long-term care insurance (WLZ).

Tax credits (heffingskortingen) are then applied to reduce the amount of tax owed. The two main credits for employees are the algemene heffingskorting (general tax credit, up to ~€3,362) and the arbeidskorting (employment tax credit, up to ~€5,052). These credits phase out at higher income levels.

Social Contributions in the Netherlands

Unlike most countries, Dutch social contributions for employees are included within the Box 1 tax rate. The 36.97% Band 1 rate breaks down approximately as:

Inkomstenbelasting (Income Tax)
9.32%
The pure income tax component of the Band 1 rate.
AOW (Algemene Ouderdomswet — State Pension)
17.9%
Contribution to the Dutch state pension (AOW). This is the largest component of the Band 1 rate.
ANW (Algemene Nabestaandenwet — Survivor's Benefit)
0.1%
Contribution to survivor's benefit insurance.
WLZ (Wet Langdurige Zorg — Long-term Care)
9.65%
Contribution to long-term care insurance.

Above €75,518 (Band 2), the 49.5% rate is pure income tax — social contributions are capped at the Band 1 ceiling.

Assumptions & Limitations

General tax credit only
This estimate applies only the algemene heffingskorting (general tax credit). The arbeidskorting (employment tax credit, up to ~€5,052) is not modelled, meaning actual net pay for most employees will be higher than shown.
Box 1 income only
This estimate covers Box 1 (employment) income only. Box 2 (substantial interest) and Box 3 (savings and investments) are taxed differently and are not included.
No healthcare premium
The Zorgverzekeringswet (ZVW) healthcare premium (~5.32% employer contribution) is not included as it is paid by the employer, not deducted from your gross salary.
No 30% ruling
The 30% ruling (30%-regeling) for highly skilled migrants is not modelled. If you qualify, your effective tax rate may be significantly lower.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why are Dutch tax rates so high?

The Box 1 rates (36.97% and 49.5%) look high because they include social contributions — state pension (AOW), survivor's benefit (ANW), and long-term care (WLZ). In most other countries, these are shown separately. The actual income tax component of Band 1 is only ~9.32%.

What is the arbeidskorting?

The arbeidskorting (employment tax credit) is a tax credit for people who work. It reduces the amount of income tax you owe. The maximum credit is ~€5,052 (2024), but it phases out at higher income levels. It is applied after the algemene heffingskorting.

What is the 30% ruling?

The 30% ruling (30%-regeling) is a tax advantage for highly skilled migrants recruited from abroad. It allows 30% of your salary to be paid tax-free, significantly reducing your effective tax rate. It applies for a maximum of 5 years and requires meeting specific conditions.

What is the difference between bruto and netto in the Netherlands?

Bruto salaris is your gross salary before deductions. Netto salaris is your take-home pay after Box 1 tax (which includes social contributions) and after tax credits have been applied. The gap between bruto and netto in the Netherlands can be significant, especially at higher income levels.
