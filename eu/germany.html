How German Income Tax Works

Germany uses a Lohnsteuer (wage tax) system. Your employer withholds income tax and social contributions from your gross salary each month and pays them directly to the tax authorities. The amount withheld depends on your Steuerklasse (tax class), which is assigned based on your marital status and employment situation.

Germany's income tax is progressive — the more you earn, the higher the rate on the top portion of your income. The Grundfreibetrag (basic tax-free allowance) of €11,604 (2024) means you pay no income tax on the first €11,604 of your annual income.

Social Contributions (Sozialversicherung)

In addition to income tax, German employees pay mandatory social insurance contributions. These are split between employee and employer. The employee share is approximately:

Rentenversicherung (Pension)
9.3%
State pension insurance. Contributions are capped at the Beitragsbemessungsgrenze (contribution ceiling).
Krankenversicherung (Health)
~7.3% + supplement
Statutory health insurance. The base rate is 7.3% plus a health fund-specific supplement (Zusatzbeitrag), averaging ~1.6% in 2024.
Arbeitslosenversicherung (Unemployment)
1.3%
Unemployment insurance, providing benefits if you lose your job.
Pflegeversicherung (Long-term Care)
1.7–2.0%
Long-term care insurance. Rate is slightly higher for those without children.

Total employee social contributions are approximately 20.05% of gross salary, subject to contribution ceilings.

Assumptions & Limitations

Tax Class I assumed
This estimate uses Tax Class I (Steuerklasse I), which applies to single employees with no children. Married couples, single parents, and those with multiple jobs use different tax classes with different withholding rates.
No church tax
Kirchensteuer (church tax) of 8–9% on income tax is not included. If you are a registered member of a church in Germany, this will reduce your net pay further.
Simplified progressive bands
Germany uses a mathematical formula for tax calculation. This estimator uses simplified band approximations which may differ slightly from the exact formula.
Contribution ceilings not fully modelled
Social contribution ceilings (Beitragsbemessungsgrenze) cap the amount subject to contributions. For very high salaries, actual social contributions may be lower than the flat rate estimate.
No Solidaritätszuschlag
The solidarity surcharge (5.5% on income tax) is not included. Most employees have been exempt since 2021, but it may apply to higher earners.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Grundfreibetrag?

The Grundfreibetrag is Germany's basic tax-free allowance — the amount of income you can earn before paying any income tax. For 2024, it is €11,604. Income above this threshold is taxed progressively.

What is Steuerklasse (tax class)?

Germany assigns employees a Steuerklasse (tax class I–VI) based on marital status, number of jobs, and other factors. Tax Class I applies to single employees. Married couples can choose between Class III/V or Class IV/IV. Your tax class significantly affects how much is withheld from your salary each month.

What is the difference between Brutto and Netto?

Bruttolohn is your gross salary — the total amount before any deductions. Nettolohn is your net salary — what you actually receive after income tax and social contributions have been deducted. In Germany, the gap between gross and net is significant due to high social contribution rates.

Does Germany have a solidarity surcharge?

The Solidaritätszuschlag (solidarity surcharge) was 5.5% on income tax, originally introduced to fund German reunification. Since 2021, most employees are exempt. It now only applies to higher earners (roughly those with income tax above ~€18,130 per year).

Is superannuation / pension included?

Yes — the Rentenversicherung (state pension insurance) contribution of 9.3% (employee share) is included in the social contributions estimate. This is mandatory for most employees.
