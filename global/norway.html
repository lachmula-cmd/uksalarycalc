Norway Income Tax System 2024

Norway uses a combination of a flat income tax rate (22%) and a progressive step tax (trinnskatt). The flat rate applies to income above the standard deduction (minstefradrag), while trinnskatt adds additional rates at higher income levels.

Flat Income Tax (22%)

A flat 22% rate applies to all taxable income (alminnelig inntekt) — your gross income minus the standard deduction (minstefradrag) and other allowable deductions.

Minstefradrag (Standard Deduction): Norway allows a standard deduction of 46% of gross income, with a minimum of NOK 31,800 and a maximum of NOK 109,950 (2024). This significantly reduces the taxable base for the flat 22% rate.

Trinnskatt — Step Tax 2024

Trinnskatt is a progressive surtax applied directly to gross income (not reduced by minstefradrag). It adds additional tax at higher income levels.

Trygdeavgift — Social Security Contributions

Trygdeavgift is Norway's social security contribution, paid by employees on gross income. It funds the National Insurance Scheme (Folketrygden), which covers state pension, health services, unemployment benefits, and parental leave.

Trygdeavgift — Employee Rate
Applies to most employment income. Funds the National Insurance Scheme (Folketrygden) including state pension (alderspensjon), health services, and unemployment benefits. No upper ceiling.
7.9%
Employer contributions: Norwegian employers pay a payroll tax (arbeidsgiveravgift) of 14.1% (standard rate) on top of your gross salary. This is not deducted from your pay but significantly increases the total cost of employment. Rates vary by region — lower rates apply in northern Norway to encourage employment.

Key Facts About the Norwegian Tax System

  • Norway's tax year runs from 1 January to 31 December.
  • Tax is deducted at source (skattetrekk) by your employer each month based on a tax deduction card (skattekort) issued by Skatteetaten.
  • Norway has a pre-filled tax return (skattemelding) — most employees simply verify and submit it online each April.
  • Norway is not an EU member but is part of the EEA (European Economic Area) and the Schengen Area.
  • Norway has one of the highest standards of living in the world, supported by oil revenues managed through the Government Pension Fund Global (Oljefondet).
  • The Norwegian krone (NOK) is not pegged to the euro. Exchange rates affect purchasing power for international comparisons.
  • Pension contributions: Most employees are enrolled in an occupational pension scheme (OTP) — employers must contribute at least 2% of salary.

Frequently Asked Questions — Norway Tax

What is the minstefradrag?

Minstefradrag is Norway's standard deduction — a flat deduction from gross income before the 22% flat tax is applied. In 2024, it is 46% of gross income, with a minimum of NOK 31,800 and a maximum of NOK 109,950. It is designed to simplify tax calculations and reduce the burden on lower earners. You cannot claim both minstefradrag and actual expenses — you choose whichever is higher.

What is trinnskatt?

Trinnskatt (step tax) is a progressive surtax applied directly to gross income. Unlike the flat 22% rate (which applies to income after minstefradrag), trinnskatt applies to your full gross salary above the thresholds. It was introduced in 2016 to replace the old "toppskatt" system. The rates range from 1.7% to 16.6% depending on income level.

How does the Norwegian tax return work?

Skatteetaten pre-fills your tax return (skattemelding) each year using data from your employer, bank, and other sources. You receive it in April and have until 30 April to review, correct, and submit it. Most employees find their return is already correct and simply confirm it. Any tax owed or refund due is settled in June.

Is Norway expensive to live in?

Yes — Norway consistently ranks among the most expensive countries in the world. However, wages are also very high, and the welfare state provides free healthcare, heavily subsidised childcare, and free university education. The effective purchasing power for residents is generally very good despite high nominal prices.

How does Norway compare to Sweden or Denmark for take-home pay?

Norway, Sweden, and Denmark all have high tax rates and strong welfare states. Norway's effective tax rates are broadly similar to Sweden's but slightly lower than Denmark's at most income levels. All three countries offer high net salaries in absolute terms due to high gross wages. See our guide on why net pay differs by country.
