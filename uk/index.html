How UK Income Tax Works

The UK uses the PAYE (Pay As You Earn) system. Your employer deducts Income Tax and National Insurance directly from your salary each pay period and pays it to HMRC on your behalf. You receive your net pay — the amount left after these deductions.

National Insurance (NI)

National Insurance is a separate contribution from Income Tax. It funds state benefits including the State Pension, NHS, and unemployment support. For employees in 2025/26:

EarningsEmployee NI Rate
Up to £12,570 (Primary Threshold)0%
£12,571 – £50,270 (Upper Earnings Limit)8%
Above £50,2702%
Note: Our calculators currently show Income Tax only. National Insurance, pension contributions, student loan repayments, and other deductions are not included in the net pay estimate. Your actual take-home pay will be lower if these apply to you.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Personal Allowance for 2025/26?

The standard Personal Allowance for 2025/26 is £12,570. This is the amount of income you can earn before paying Income Tax. It is reduced by £1 for every £2 you earn above £100,000, and reaches £0 at £125,140.

Does Scotland have different income tax rates?

Yes. Scotland has its own income tax rates set by the Scottish Parliament. Scottish taxpayers have more bands (including a 19% Starter Rate and a 21% Intermediate Rate) and higher top rates (42%, 45%, and 48%) compared to the rest of the UK. Our calculator includes both England/Wales and Scotland options.

Is National Insurance included in the calculator?

Currently, our UK calculator shows Income Tax only. National Insurance is a separate deduction — employees pay 8% on earnings between £12,570 and £50,270, and 2% above that. Your actual take-home pay will be lower once NI is deducted.

What is the difference between gross and net salary?

Your gross salary is your total pay before any deductions. Your net salary (take-home pay) is what you receive after Income Tax, National Insurance, and any other deductions have been taken. See our Gross vs Net Salary guide for a full explanation.

How do I convert my salary to an hourly rate?

Divide your annual salary by the number of weeks you work per year, then divide by your hours per week. For example: £30,000 ÷ 52 weeks ÷ 37.5 hours = £15.38 per hour. Our UK Salary Calculator does this automatically.
