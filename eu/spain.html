How Spanish Income Tax Works

Spain uses the IRPF (Impuesto sobre la Renta de las Personas Físicas) — a personal income tax system. IRPF is split between a state portion and a regional portion, with each of Spain's 17 autonomous communities setting their own regional rates. The rates shown here are combined state + average regional rates.

Your employer withholds IRPF from your monthly salary (retención a cuenta) based on an estimated annual rate. The final amount is reconciled in your annual tax return (declaración de la renta), filed between April and June of the following year.

A mínimo personal (personal minimum) of €5,550 reduces your taxable base. Additional allowances apply for age, disability, and dependants.

Social Security Contributions (Seguridad Social)

Spanish employees pay Social Security contributions on their gross salary. The employee contribution rate is approximately 6.35%, broken down as:

Contingencias comunes (Pension & Health)
4.7%
The main Social Security contribution covering pension, health, and other common contingencies.
Desempleo (Unemployment)
1.55%
Unemployment insurance contribution for employees on permanent contracts.
Formación profesional (Vocational Training)
0.1%
Contribution to vocational training fund.

Social Security contributions are subject to a base máxima (contribution ceiling) of approximately €4,720.50/month (2024). Earnings above this ceiling are not subject to Social Security contributions.

Note: Employers also pay significant Social Security contributions (approximately 29.9% of gross salary). These are not deducted from your salary but represent an additional cost to your employer on top of your gross pay.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is IRPF?

IRPF (Impuesto sobre la Renta de las Personas Físicas) is Spain's personal income tax. It is a progressive tax applied to employment income, with rates split between the state and each autonomous community. Your employer withholds an estimated amount each month (retención), which is reconciled in your annual tax return.

Do Spanish tax rates vary by region?

Yes. Spain's 17 autonomous communities each set their own regional IRPF rates. Madrid is known for having lower regional rates, while Catalonia and other regions have higher rates. The difference can be meaningful for higher earners. This estimate uses average combined rates.

What is the mínimo personal?

The mínimo personal (€5,550 for 2024) is a personal minimum allowance that reduces your taxable base. Additional allowances apply for age (over 65 or 75), disability, and dependants (children, elderly parents). These reduce the amount of income subject to IRPF.

Why do some Spanish employees receive 14 salary payments?

Many Spanish employment contracts include two extra monthly payments — one in summer (typically July) and one at Christmas (December). These are called pagas extraordinarias. The annual gross salary is divided into 14 payments rather than 12. Some employers integrate these into 12 monthly payments instead.

What is the difference between salario bruto and salario neto?

Salario bruto is your gross salary before any deductions. Salario neto is your take-home pay after IRPF income tax and Social Security contributions have been deducted. In Spain, the gap between bruto and neto depends heavily on your income level and autonomous community.
