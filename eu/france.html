How French Income Tax Works

France uses a system called impôt sur le revenu (IR) — income tax assessed annually on your household's total income. Unlike the UK's PAYE system, French income tax is calculated on the foyer fiscal (tax household), which can include a spouse and dependants. The number of "parts" in your household (quotient familial) affects how much tax you pay.

Since 2019, France has introduced prélèvement à la source — withholding at source — meaning your employer now deducts an estimated income tax amount from your monthly salary, similar to PAYE. The final amount is reconciled in your annual tax return.

A standard 10% employment deduction (abattement forfaitaire) is applied to employment income before calculating tax, with a minimum of €495 and a maximum of €14,171.

Social Contributions (Cotisations Sociales)

French employees pay significant social contributions on top of income tax. These fund health insurance, pension, unemployment, and other social benefits. The main employee contributions are:

CSG (Contribution Sociale Généralisée)
9.2%
The main social solidarity contribution. Applied to 98.25% of gross salary. Partially deductible from income tax.
CRDS (Contribution au Remboursement de la Dette Sociale)
0.5%
Contribution to repay social debt. Applied to 98.25% of gross salary. Not deductible from income tax.
Assurance maladie (Health)
~0.75%
Employee health insurance contribution (reduced rate since 2018 reform).
Retraite complémentaire (Supplementary Pension)
~3.93–4.72%
AGIRC-ARRCO supplementary pension contributions. Rate depends on salary level relative to the PASS (plafond annuel de la Sécurité sociale).
Prévoyance (Disability/Death)
~0.5–1%
Supplementary disability and death insurance. Rate varies by employer agreement.

Total employee social contributions are approximately 22% of gross salary, though the exact rate depends on salary level and employer agreement.

Assumptions & Limitations

Single person assumed
This estimate assumes a single employed person with no dependants (1 part in the quotient familial). Married couples and those with children will have a different tax calculation.
10% employment deduction applied
The standard 10% abattement forfaitaire is applied. If you have higher actual professional expenses, you may be able to deduct more.
Flat social contribution rate
Social contributions are estimated at a flat ~22% rate. Actual rates vary by salary level, employer agreement, and specific contribution type.
No regional or local taxes
France does not have significant regional income taxes for employees. The estimate covers national income tax and social contributions only.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the quotient familial?

The quotient familial is France's system for adjusting income tax based on household size. A single person has 1 part. A married couple has 2 parts. Each dependent child adds 0.5 parts. Income is divided by the number of parts, tax is calculated, then multiplied back — this reduces the effective tax rate for larger households.

What is the difference between salaire brut and salaire net?

Salaire brut is your gross salary before any deductions. Salaire net is your take-home pay after social contributions and income tax. In France, the gap is significant — social contributions alone are around 22% of gross salary for employees.

What is prélèvement à la source?

Since January 2019, France has operated a withholding-at-source system (prélèvement à la source). Your employer deducts an estimated income tax amount from your monthly salary based on a rate provided by the tax authority (DGFiP). The final amount is reconciled when you file your annual tax return.

Is CSG deductible from income tax?

Part of the CSG (6.8% out of 9.2%) is deductible from your taxable income for income tax purposes. This partially offsets the impact of CSG on your overall tax burden. Our simplified estimate does not model this deductibility.
