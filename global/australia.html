How Australian Income Tax Works

Australia uses a PAYG (Pay As You Go) withholding system, administered by the Australian Taxation Office (ATO). Your employer withholds income tax from each pay period based on your Tax File Number (TFN) declaration. You lodge an annual tax return (typically by 31 October) to reconcile the actual amount owed.

Australia has a tax-free threshold of A$18,200 — the first A$18,200 of income is not subject to income tax. Above this, progressive rates apply.

The Medicare Levy (2%) is a separate charge that funds Australia's public healthcare system (Medicare). It applies to most taxpayers above a low-income threshold.

Superannuation: Australia's compulsory retirement savings system (Superannuation) requires employers to contribute 11.5% of your gross salary into a super fund. This is an employer cost paid on top of your gross salary — it is not deducted from your take-home pay. It is a significant benefit that makes Australian total compensation higher than the gross salary figure suggests.

Medicare Levy

The Medicare Levy is a 2% charge on taxable income that funds Australia's public healthcare system. It is separate from income tax and applies to most taxpayers.

Medicare Levy
2%
Applies to most Australian residents. A Medicare Levy reduction or exemption applies to low-income earners (below approximately A$26,000 for singles in 2024–25). The Medicare Levy Surcharge (1%–1.5%) applies to higher earners without private hospital cover — not included in this estimate.

Superannuation (Super)

Superannuation is Australia's compulsory retirement savings system. Key facts for 2024–25:

Superannuation Guarantee (SG) Rate
11.5%
Employers must contribute 11.5% of your ordinary time earnings into a complying super fund. This is paid on top of your gross salary — it is not deducted from your take-home pay. The rate increases to 12% from 1 July 2025.
Why this matters for comparisons: When comparing Australian salaries to other countries, remember that the employer super contribution (11.5%) is an additional benefit on top of gross salary. A A$80,000 salary in Australia effectively comes with A$9,200 in employer super contributions — making the total employment cost A$89,200.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the tax-free threshold in Australia?

The tax-free threshold for 2024–25 is A$18,200. Australian residents do not pay income tax on the first A$18,200 of income. You can claim the tax-free threshold from your employer by completing a Tax File Number (TFN) declaration.

Is Superannuation deducted from my salary?

No. Superannuation (11.5% for 2024–25) is paid by your employer on top of your gross salary — it is not deducted from your take-home pay. When a job advertises a salary of A$80,000, your employer also pays A$9,200 into your super fund separately. Some job ads show "total package" which includes super — always check whether the advertised salary is inclusive or exclusive of super.

What is the Medicare Levy?

The Medicare Levy is a 2% charge on taxable income that funds Australia's public healthcare system (Medicare). It is separate from income tax. Most Australian residents pay it. A Medicare Levy Surcharge (1%–1.5%) also applies to higher earners who do not have private hospital cover.

What changed with the Stage 3 tax cuts?

The Stage 3 tax cuts, effective from 1 July 2024, restructured Australia's income tax bands. The 37% band now starts at A$120,000 (previously A$135,000), and the 32.5% band was extended. The 19% rate now applies from A$18,201 to A$45,000. These changes benefit most middle-income earners.
