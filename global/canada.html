How Canadian Federal Income Tax Works

Canada uses a progressive federal income tax system administered by the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA). Your employer deducts federal income tax from each paycheque based on your TD1 form. You file an annual tax return (T1) by April 30 to reconcile the actual amount owed.

The Basic Personal Amount (BPA) of CA$15,705 for 2024 is a non-refundable tax credit that effectively makes the first ~CA$15,705 of income tax-free at the federal level.

In addition to federal income tax, employees pay CPP (Canada Pension Plan) and EI (Employment Insurance) contributions. These are mandatory for most employed Canadians.

Provincial taxes not included: Each Canadian province and territory levies its own income tax on top of federal tax. Combined federal + provincial rates range from approximately 20% (low income in Alberta) to over 53% (high income in Nova Scotia or Quebec). Your actual take-home pay will be significantly lower once provincial tax is added.

CPP and EI Contributions

In addition to income tax, Canadian employees pay two mandatory contributions:

Canada Pension Plan (CPP)
5.95%
Funds retirement, disability, and survivor benefits. Applied to earnings between the basic exemption (CA$3,500) and the Year's Maximum Pensionable Earnings (YMPE) ceiling of CA$68,500 for 2024. Maximum annual contribution: approximately CA$3,867.
Employment Insurance (EI)
1.66%
Funds unemployment benefits and parental leave. Applied to insurable earnings up to the maximum insurable earnings ceiling of CA$63,200 for 2024. Maximum annual premium: approximately CA$1,049.

Employers also contribute to CPP (matching employee contributions) and EI (1.4× the employee premium). These are employer costs on top of your gross salary.

Assumptions & Limitations

Federal tax only
Provincial income taxes are not included. Every province and territory levies additional income tax. Combined federal + provincial rates can reach 53%+ in some provinces at high income levels.
Basic Personal Amount applied as credit
The BPA (CA$15,705) is applied as a non-refundable tax credit at 15%, reducing federal tax by CA$2,356. Other non-refundable credits (CPP credit, EI credit) are not modelled.
Employment income only
This estimate assumes all income is from employment. Investment income, capital gains, self-employment income, and RRSP contributions are not modelled.
CPP2 not included
Canada introduced CPP2 (a second additional CPP contribution) in 2024 for earnings between CA$68,500 and CA$73,200. This is not included in the estimate.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Basic Personal Amount?

The Basic Personal Amount (BPA) is a non-refundable tax credit that reduces your federal income tax. For 2024, it is CA$15,705. It is applied at the 15% rate, reducing your federal tax by CA$2,356. It effectively makes the first ~CA$15,705 of income tax-free at the federal level.

What is CPP?

The Canada Pension Plan (CPP) is a mandatory contributory pension plan. Employees contribute 5.95% of pensionable earnings (between CA$3,500 and CA$68,500 in 2024). Employers match this contribution. CPP provides retirement, disability, and survivor benefits.

What is EI?

Employment Insurance (EI) provides temporary income support if you lose your job, are sick, pregnant, or caring for a newborn or seriously ill family member. Employees pay 1.66% of insurable earnings up to CA$63,200 (2024). Employers pay 1.4× the employee premium.

How much is provincial income tax in Canada?

Provincial income tax varies significantly. At CA$70,000 gross, approximate provincial tax ranges from about CA$5,000 (Alberta) to CA$10,000+ (Quebec, Nova Scotia). Combined federal + provincial effective rates at this income level typically range from 25% to 35% depending on province.
