Italy IRPEF Income Tax Bands 2024

Italy's income tax (IRPEF — Imposta sul Reddito delle Persone Fisiche) uses a progressive rate system. The 2024 reform consolidated the previous four bands into three, reducing the tax burden for many middle-income earners.

Band Rate Income Range Notes
Band 1 23% €0 – €28,000 Standard rate band
Band 2 35% €28,001 – €50,000 Middle rate band
Band 3 43% Above €50,000 Top rate band
Employment deductions (detrazioni per lavoro dipendente): Employees receive a tax deduction that reduces their IRPEF liability. The deduction is approximately €1,880 for lower incomes and tapers off as income rises, reaching zero above €50,000. This is applied automatically by your employer.
Regional and municipal taxes: In addition to national IRPEF, Italian residents pay addizionale regionale (regional surcharge, typically 1.23–3.33%) and addizionale comunale (municipal surcharge, up to 0.9%). These are not included in this estimator but can add 1.5–4% to your total tax burden.

INPS — Social Security Contributions

Italian employees pay INPS (Istituto Nazionale della Previdenza Sociale) contributions, which fund the state pension, illness benefits, and other social protections. The employee contribution rate varies by employment category.

INPS — Employee Pension Contribution
The main employee social contribution. Funds the state pension (pensione) and other INPS benefits. Rate applies up to a contribution ceiling (massimale contributivo).
~9.19%
INPS — Employer Contribution
Employers pay approximately 23–30% of gross salary in INPS contributions. This is not deducted from your salary but significantly increases the total cost of employment.
~23–30%
Note on RAL vs. busta paga: In Italy, your gross salary is often quoted as RAL (Reddito Annuo Lordo). Your monthly payslip (busta paga) will show deductions for IRPEF, INPS, and any regional/municipal taxes. The net figure on your payslip is your take-home pay.

Key Facts About the Italian Tax System

23–43% IRPEF Rates
~9.19% Employee INPS
~23–30% Employer INPS
+1.5–4% Regional/Municipal Tax
  • Italy's tax year runs from 1 January to 31 December.
  • IRPEF is withheld monthly by your employer (sostituto d'imposta) via the PAYE-equivalent system.
  • The 2024 reform reduced IRPEF from four bands to three, benefiting middle-income earners.
  • Italy has one of the highest employer social contribution rates in the EU (23–30%).
  • Employees receive 13 or 14 monthly salary payments per year in many sectors (tredicesima/quattordicesima).
  • TFR (Trattamento di Fine Rapporto) — a severance fund — is accrued at ~6.91% of gross salary per year and paid on leaving employment.
  • Regional taxes vary significantly: residents of Lombardy pay less than those in Calabria or Lazio.

Frequently Asked Questions — Italy Tax

What is RAL in Italy?

RAL stands for Reddito Annuo Lordo — your annual gross salary before any deductions. When Italian employers advertise salaries, they typically quote the RAL. Your actual take-home pay (netto) will be significantly lower after IRPEF, INPS, and regional taxes are deducted.

Why is Italian take-home pay lower than in other EU countries?

Italy has relatively high social contribution rates and progressive income tax bands that start at 23%. Combined with regional and municipal surcharges, the total effective deduction rate for a €40,000 salary can exceed 30–35%. High employer contributions also make Italy an expensive country to hire in.

What is the tredicesima (13th month salary)?

Most Italian employees receive a 13th monthly salary payment (tredicesima) in December, equivalent to one month's gross pay. Some sectors also pay a 14th salary (quattordicesima) in June or July. These are included in your RAL and are subject to normal IRPEF and INPS deductions.

What is TFR?

TFR (Trattamento di Fine Rapporto) is a mandatory severance fund. Your employer sets aside approximately 6.91% of your gross salary each year. You receive this lump sum when you leave the company (resignation, redundancy, or retirement). You can also choose to direct TFR contributions to a pension fund.

How does Italy compare to Germany or France for take-home pay?

Italy generally has lower net salaries than Germany or the Netherlands at equivalent gross levels, due to higher combined deduction rates and lower average wages. However, Italy's cost of living varies significantly by region. See our guide on why net pay differs by country for a broader comparison.
