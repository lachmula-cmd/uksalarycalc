Ireland Income Tax Bands 2024 (PAYE)

Ireland uses a two-rate income tax system. The standard rate applies up to a threshold, with the higher rate applying above it. The threshold depends on your personal circumstances (single, married, etc.).

Band Rate Income Range (Single) Notes
Standard Rate Band 20% €0 – €42,000 Higher for married couples
Higher Rate 40% Above €42,000 Applies to income above the standard rate band
Tax Credits: Ireland uses a tax credit system rather than a tax-free allowance. A single employee typically receives a Personal Tax Credit (€1,875) and an Employee Tax Credit (€1,875), totalling €3,750 per year. These are deducted directly from your tax bill — not from your income.

Universal Social Charge (USC) 2024

The USC is a separate charge on gross income (before pension contributions). It applies to most income earners. Those earning under €13,000 per year are exempt from USC.

USC Band Rate Income Range
Band 1 0.5% €0 – €12,012
Band 2 2% €12,013 – €25,760
Band 3 4% €25,761 – €70,044
Band 4 8% Above €70,044

A reduced rate of 2% applies to income above €13,000 for those aged 70+ or with a full medical card.

PRSI — Pay Related Social Insurance

PRSI is Ireland's social insurance contribution. Most employees pay Class A PRSI, which funds state pension, jobseeker's benefit, illness benefit, and other social welfare payments.

PRSI — Class A (Employee)
Applies to most employees. Funds state pension, illness benefit, maternity benefit, and jobseeker's benefit. No ceiling on PRSI contributions.
4.1%
PRSI — Employer Contribution
Employers pay an additional 11.15% PRSI on employee earnings. This is not deducted from your salary but is a cost to your employer.
11.15%
PRSI exemption: Employees earning less than €352 per week (€18,304 per year) are exempt from employee PRSI contributions.

Key Facts About the Irish Tax System

20% / 40% Income Tax Rates
€3,750 Standard Tax Credits
0.5–8% USC Bands
4.1% Employee PRSI
  • Ireland's tax year runs from 1 January to 31 December.
  • Tax is collected under the PAYE system — deducted at source by your employer.
  • The USC is charged on gross income, before pension contributions are deducted.
  • PRSI Class A applies to most private sector employees earning over €352/week.
  • Married couples and civil partners can transfer unused standard rate band between spouses.
  • Pension contributions (occupational or PRSA) reduce your taxable income for PAYE purposes.
  • Ireland has a relatively high employer PRSI rate (11.15%), making total employment costs significantly higher than the employee's gross salary.

Frequently Asked Questions — Ireland Tax

What is the difference between PAYE, USC, and PRSI?

PAYE (Pay As You Earn) is income tax — the main tax on your earnings. USC (Universal Social Charge) is a separate charge on gross income introduced in 2011. PRSI (Pay Related Social Insurance) funds social welfare benefits like the state pension and illness benefit. All three are deducted from your salary by your employer.

How do Irish tax credits work?

Unlike a tax-free allowance, Irish tax credits reduce your tax bill directly. A single employee typically receives a Personal Tax Credit (€1,875) and an Employee Tax Credit (€1,875), totalling €3,750. This means the first €18,750 of income is effectively tax-free (€3,750 ÷ 20% = €18,750).

Is USC the same as income tax?

No. USC is a separate charge calculated on gross income before pension deductions. It has its own rate bands (0.5% to 8%) and is charged in addition to income tax. You pay both PAYE and USC on most employment income.

Do I pay PRSI on all my income?

Class A PRSI applies to most employment income with no upper ceiling. However, employees earning less than €352 per week are exempt from employee PRSI. Self-employed individuals pay Class S PRSI at 4%.

How does Ireland compare to the UK for take-home pay?

Ireland and the UK have broadly similar effective tax rates at mid-range salaries, but the structures differ significantly. Ireland has three separate deductions (PAYE, USC, PRSI) while the UK uses Income Tax and National Insurance. At higher salaries, Ireland's 40% rate kicks in at a lower threshold than the UK's 40% rate. See our guide on why net pay differs by country.
